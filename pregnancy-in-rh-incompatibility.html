Pregnancy In RH Incompatibility

Rh incompatibility during pregnancy is a form of anemia wherein the mother's immune system targets and destroys the red blood cells of the fetus due to a disparity in blood types between the mother and the child, specifically if the mother has Rh-negative blood and the baby has Rh-positive blood.

During pregnancy, especially during delivery, the baby's blood can enter the mother's bloodstream. In cases where the mother is Rh-negative and the baby is Rh-positive, the mother's body interprets the baby's blood as foreign.

As a result, the mother's body generates antibodies (proteins) against the Rh-positive blood of the baby. Typically, these antibodies do not pose a significant threat during the first pregnancy because the baby is often born before many antibodies are produced.

However, once these antibodies are created, they remain in the mother's body. Consequently, Rh incompatibility in pregnancy is more likely to cause complications in subsequent pregnancies if the baby is Rh-positive.

These Rh antibodies have the potential to traverse the placenta and assault the baby's red blood cells. This can result in hemolytic anemia in the baby, which leads to a shortage of red blood cells and a reduced oxygen supply for the baby. In severe cases, severe hemolytic anemia can even prove fatal for the child.

The good news is that, with timely and appropriate prenatal care and screening, the issues related to Rh incompatibility treatment can be mitigated. Screening tests enable your healthcare provider to determine early in your pregnancy whether you are at risk for this condition.

If you are identified as being at risk, your healthcare provider will diligently monitor both you and your baby throughout the pregnancy and prescribe the necessary treatment.
